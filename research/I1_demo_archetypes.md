# Winning City-Hall Hackathons: Timing-Tight Demo Playbooks that Judges Trust

## Executive Summary
Hackathon judges evaluate civic tech projects under extreme time constraints, typically deciding a project's viability within the first 60 seconds of a 3-to-5-minute pitch [1]. For City Hall tools, the core challenge is balancing complex background context (like miscategorized 311 requests or buried procurement data) with a live, credible demonstration of the solution. 

Our analysis reveals that matching the presentation archetype to the specific civic problem is the strongest predictor of success. For service navigation tools (like 311 routing), the **"Before and After"** archetype performs best by visually contrasting resident frustration with a streamlined solution. For procurement and transparency dashboards, the **"Problem Then Proof"** archetype dominates by anchoring the pitch in hard financial data and verifiable metrics. Across all archetypes, maintaining strict timing and establishing data credibility are non-negotiable; overrunning the clock or overclaiming capabilities frequently leads to disqualification or severe penalty [2] [3].

## 1. Why Demo Structure Makes or Breaks City-Hall Pitches
Judges at civic hackathons process information in a highly predictable sequence. According to pitch evaluators, the first 15 seconds determine if the project is interesting, the next 45 seconds establish if the problem matters, and the following minute proves whether the solution actually works [1]. Judges are explicitly looking for "Problem. Solution. Proof. Potential" rather than deep technical implementation details [1].

Furthermore, standard civic hackathon rubrics weight the clarity of the problem and solution equally to the completion stage of the project [4]. A stunning presentation can elevate an average project, provided the presenter can clearly articulate the business case and execute a flawless demo within the strict 3-to-5-minute window [2].

## 2. Archetype Performance Matrix for City-Hall Problem Types
Selecting the right narrative structure is critical. The table below assesses how the three primary demo archetypes perform across the two main City Hall problem domains.

| Archetype | Service Navigation (311) Fit | Procurement Dashboard Fit | Core Strength | Primary Risk | Best Evidence Hook |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Before & After** | **High (Recommended)** | Low | High visual contrast of user pain | Live demo failure during transition | Time-to-complete reduction |
| **Problem then Proof** | Medium | **High (Recommended)** | Establishes immediate data credibility | Relying on unverified or exaggerated stats | Dollar value of waste/leakage |
| **User Journey** | Medium | Medium | Creates a relatable, human story | Persona becomes too wordy or unrealistic | Persona combined with a KPI |

*Takeaway:* Align your archetype with the judge's cognitive trigger. Use emotive, visual contrasts for resident-facing tools, and hard, numeric anchors for budget-facing tools.

## 3. Deep Dive: Crafting a 311 Routing Demo
For service navigation tools like 311 routing helpers, the **"Before and After"** archetype is highly recommended. The current experience for residents is often visibly frustrating, making the contrast with your streamlined solution immediately impactful.

### 3.1 Hooking with Resident Pain
Begin by showing the painful current experience. Spend no more than 30 seconds demonstrating a resident struggling to navigate a convoluted city website (e.g., hitting dead ends when reporting illegal dumping). This visually establishes the problem without requiring lengthy verbal explanations.

### 3.2 The Live Routing Walkthrough
Transition immediately to your solution. Spend 60 seconds showing the same resident using your tool to successfully route the request. The contrast must be stark and the interaction must be live. 

### 3.3 Quantifying the Difference
Conclude the demo by quantifying the impact. Use credible data points, such as estimating the percentage of miscategorized requests based on historical 311 volume, and clearly state how your tool reduces this error rate. Ensure all claims are honest and not overclaimed to maintain credibility.

## 4. Deep Dive: Contract Dashboard Demo
For procurement and open contracting tools, the **"Problem Then Proof"** archetype is the optimal choice. Procurement is inherently data-heavy, and judges (often including industry experts or city officials) need to see verifiable financial impact [2].

### 4.1 Sourcing the Anchor Metric
Start by stating the problem with a massive, credible data point. For example, reference official reports on procurement delays or lack of competition. Case studies show that transparent procurement practices, such as open data portals and e-auctions, can significantly impact costs; for instance, the use of e-auctions in Slovak municipalities was linked to an average decrease in winning prices by approximately 2.4% of the expected price [5]. 

### 4.2 The 3-Click KPI Tour
Move directly into the solution by showing the dashboard. Focus on how the tool surfaces critical KPIs, such as supplier diversity metrics or compliance tracking, similar to successful implementations by organizations like IBM and Siemens [6]. In New York City, transparent contract tracking systems are advocated to help vendors avoid the "black hole" of contract review [7]. Show exactly how your tool illuminates these dark spots in 3 clicks or less.

### 4.3 Risk Call-outs & Legal Disclaimers
Procurement data is sensitive. Explicitly state your data sources and include proper disclaimers (e.g., "using a demo subset of 2024 CAFR data"). This separates your civic tech solution from vaporware and builds immediate trust with the judging panel.

## 5. Five-Minute Timing Playbooks
Time management is the most common failure point in hackathon pitches. A 3-minute pitch allows for roughly 390-420 words [8]. Overrunning your allotted time looks unprofessional and can severely damage your chances of winning [2]. 

| Time Stamp | Before & After (311 Focus) | Problem then Proof (Procurement Focus) | User Journey (Alternative) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **0–15 s** | Resident pain visual hook | Shocking data/stat hook | Relatable persona intro |
| **15–60 s** | Full "Before" struggle clip | Contextualize the financial problem | Establish the user's specific challenge |
| **60–120 s** | Switch to live "After" solution | Live dashboard/tool demo | Live journey through the tool |
| **120–210 s** | Quantify time/clicks saved | Quantify financial/efficiency benefit | Highlight KPI improvements |
| **210–250 s** | Credibility slide (Data sources) | Credibility slide (Disclaimers) | Credibility slide (Feasibility) |
| **250–270 s** | Clear ask & call-to-action | Clear ask & call-to-action | Clear ask & call-to-action |
| **270–300 s** | Buffer / Transition to Q&A | Buffer / Transition to Q&A | Buffer / Transition to Q&A |

*Takeaway:* Script your presentation down to the second. If you fail to finish within the time limit during practice, cut content immediately [8].

## 6. Judge Psychology & Scoring Criteria
Judges are evaluating whether the project has reached a stage of completion and if the problem and solution are clearly defined [4]. They are not thinking about how many hours you spent coding or the internal team dynamics; they care about the problem, the solution, the proof, and the potential [1]. If you have the opportunity, asking to go last can make your demo more memorable after judges have sat through numerous other presentations [9].

## 7. Risk Mitigation: Avoiding Vaporware & Data Pitfalls
Credibility is the currency of civic tech. Hackathon teams have been disqualified for misrepresenting what their hacks actually accomplished [3]. Furthermore, civic technologies must inspire trust to be adopted; issues like data privacy, pseudonymity, and the potential for political reuse of data are major concerns for users and institutions alike [10]. Always use on-screen data source callouts and explicitly state when you are using simulated or subset data to preempt any doubts about the legitimacy of your build.

## 8. Rehearsal & Delivery Toolkit
Do not start coding until you have planned your presentation. Spend the first hour of the hackathon figuring out exactly what your demo will look like, choosing one or two key workflows to present [2]. Brainstorm your storyline with Post-its before touching presentation software [8]. Finally, practice the presentation until it perfectly fits the time limit, making it a group effort to track time and refine the delivery [2].

## 9. Action Checklist & Templates
* **Select Archetype:** Choose "Before & After" for resident tools; "Problem Then Proof" for internal/financial tools.
* **Draft the Script:** Limit to 400 words for a 3-minute pitch [8].
* **Verify Data:** Ensure all anchor metrics are cited from official city reports or credible case studies.
* **Add Disclaimers:** Insert a "Data Source & Limitations" slide to build trust.
* **Rehearse:** Run the full pitch with a timer at least 5 times, ensuring a hard stop at the 4:30 mark to leave room for Q&A.
