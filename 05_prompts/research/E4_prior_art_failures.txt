Prior Art E4 — Failed Government Chatbot and Service Tools: Why They Failed

Investigate documented cases of government service chatbots, AI assistants, and digital service tools that failed — either technically, in terms of user adoption, or in terms of causing harm through incorrect information. This is critical for avoiding the same mistakes in a Richmond prototype.

Failure categories to investigate:
1. CONFIDENTLY WRONG ANSWERS: Cases where government chatbots gave incorrect service routing, eligibility information, or benefit guidance — and the consequences for residents and agencies
2. TRUST EROSION: Cases where tools that initially seemed helpful created long-term distrust (e.g., benefits chatbots that gave wrong denial reasons)
3. ABANDONMENT: Government digital tools that were built but never adopted by staff or residents — what were the root causes?
4. SCOPE CREEP: Tools that worked for simple queries but failed on edge cases, creating a worse experience than no tool at all
5. DATA STALENESS: Tools that gave outdated information because the underlying content wasn't maintained

Specific examples to investigate:
- HHS benefit finder failures
- State unemployment chatbot failures during COVID-19
- Municipal chatbot deployments that were pulled back
- Code for America post-mortems

Also investigate: What do UX researchers and civic technologists say are the most common design mistakes in government digital service tools?

Output
- Documented failure cases with root causes
- Design principles derived from failures
- Specific language and architecture patterns that contributed to failures
- How to communicate limitations to judges without undermining the prototype's credibility
