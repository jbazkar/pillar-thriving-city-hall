Opportunities F4 — Where Software Helps vs. Where It Doesn't

Investigate the specific places where software can genuinely improve city service delivery and procurement — and the places where software intervention is inappropriate, ineffective, or actively harmful.

WHERE SOFTWARE HELPS (for A Thriving City Hall):
- Information retrieval and plain-language presentation of static content
- Pattern recognition in structured data (contract expiry dates, category volumes)
- Decision support tools that surface information for human review
- Reducing cognitive load for routine, well-defined tasks
- Making public data accessible without requiring specialized knowledge

WHERE SOFTWARE DOESN'T HELP OR CAUSES HARM:
- Situations requiring human judgment, context, or empathy
- Routing decisions for complex, multi-factor situations
- Determinations with legal or financial consequences
- Situations where the underlying process is broken (software can't fix bad policy)
- Edge cases that fall outside the system's training or rule set

For the A Thriving City Hall problems specifically:
1. SERVICE NAVIGATION: Which resident situations are genuinely software-amenable vs. which require a trained 311 agent? Where is the boundary?
2. PROCUREMENT: Which contract review tasks are software-amenable vs. which require a trained procurement officer? Where is the boundary?

What do civic technologists and government IT professionals say about the appropriate scope of automation in public services?

Output
- Specific do/don't map for service navigation software
- Specific do/don't map for procurement software
- Design principles for staying in the "appropriate software role" zone
- Language for explaining these limits in a hackathon demo
