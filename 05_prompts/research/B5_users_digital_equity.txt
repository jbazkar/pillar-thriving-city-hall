User & Stakeholder B5 — Digital Equity for Richmond Residents

Investigate the digital equity context for resident-facing City Hall tools in Richmond. A plain-language service navigator that only works on desktop with high digital literacy serves only a subset of residents who need help most.

Richmond demographic context: What are Richmond's digital equity indicators — broadband access rates, smartphone-only household rates, digital literacy distributions, multilingual population percentages? How do these vary by neighborhood?

Device and access patterns: What proportion of Richmond residents are mobile-only internet users? What does this mean for 311 submission tools (mobile web vs. app vs. phone)?

Language access: rva.gov uses Google Translate for 36 languages. What are the limitations of Google Translate for government service terminology? Which languages are most commonly needed in Richmond?

Low-literacy residents: What design patterns help residents with limited literacy navigate government service tools? What are the most effective approaches for reducing cognitive load in service navigation?

Seniors and older residents: What specific barriers do older Richmond residents face with digital City service tools? Are there accessibility features that are most important for this group?

Phone vs. digital channel: For residents who can't or won't use the online 311 system, what is the experience of calling 311 by phone? Is there documented equity in service quality across channels?

Output
- Facts (with URLs) on Richmond digital equity indicators
- Design requirements for an equitable resident service navigation tool
- Recommendations for ensuring a hackathon prototype doesn't create a two-tier service experience
