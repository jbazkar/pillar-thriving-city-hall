Risks G5 — Practical Guardrails: Confidence Calibration, Source Citation, and Human Review

Investigate practical guardrail design for City Hall civic tech tools. This covers three critical safety mechanisms: confidence calibration (admitting uncertainty), source citation (showing where information comes from), and human review pathways (when to escalate to staff).

CONFIDENCE CALIBRATION:
- What are the most effective UI patterns for showing confidence levels in government information tools?
- When should a service navigation tool say "I'm not sure" vs. give a best-guess answer?
- What threshold of uncertainty should trigger a "please call 311 at 3-1-1" response?
- How do well-designed tools handle queries that fall completely outside their knowledge base?
- What are the anti-patterns (tools that appear confident but shouldn't be)?

SOURCE CITATION:
- What source citation approaches work best in civic tech tools — inline citations, footnotes, "learn more" links?
- How should a tool communicate when information is from rva.gov vs. historical 311 data vs. team-created content?
- What does the "last verified" date requirement mean in practice — how should it be displayed?
- What are examples of effective source transparency in government information tools?

HUMAN REVIEW PATHWAYS:
- For service navigation: what are the best-practice "human fallback" patterns (link to 311, display phone number, "chat with staff")?
- For procurement tools: what workflow should staff follow after seeing a contract flagged by the tool?
- What is the minimum viable human escalation path that must exist in every City Hall prototype?

Output
- Guardrail design patterns for confidence, citations, and human fallback
- Required guardrail elements for any A Thriving City Hall prototype
- Example UI language and placement for each guardrail element
