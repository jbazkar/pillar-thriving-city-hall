Demo Guidance I4 — How to Establish Credibility: Real Data, Real Scenarios, Real Limits

Investigate what makes a civic tech hackathon demo credible — specifically for City Hall tools that work with government data and make information recommendations.

THE CREDIBILITY PROBLEM:
A 48-hour prototype can look polished but be fundamentally misleading — using fake data, claiming capabilities it doesn't have, or hiding its failure modes. Judges at civic hackathons are increasingly sophisticated and will probe for this.

CREDIBILITY BUILDING BLOCKS:
1. Real data: Using actual public Richmond data (Socrata, rva.gov) vs. synthetic or sample data
2. Accurate problem description: Citing actual system names (AvePoint, Dynamics 365, Drupal/Acquia) and documented problems (column bug, no post-2018 API)
3. Honest capabilities: Telling judges what the tool can and cannot do
4. Demonstrated uncertainty handling: Showing the tool saying "I'm not sure" when appropriate
5. Source attribution: Showing where every piece of information comes from

RED FLAGS JUDGES NOTICE:
- Hardcoded "great" responses that would fail on any other query
- Claiming "integration with" City systems that the team didn't actually build
- Confidence calibration that's always at 95%+
- No mention of limitations, failure modes, or edge cases
- Demo scenarios that are suspiciously perfect

BUILDING CREDIBILITY THROUGH HONESTY:
What does research say about how admitting limitations in a civic tech demo actually increases judge confidence rather than decreasing it?

How do experienced civic tech teams handle the "what doesn't it do?" question in a way that strengthens rather than weakens their pitch?

Output
- Credibility checklist: what every City Hall prototype demo must demonstrate
- Language patterns that signal honesty and build trust
- How to pre-answer the "what are the limitations?" question before judges ask it
