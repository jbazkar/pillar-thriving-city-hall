Risks G1 — Harm from Confidently Wrong Service Routing

Investigate the specific risk of a 311 helper or service navigation tool providing confidently wrong routing advice to Richmond residents. This is the highest-stakes risk for the resident service navigation problem.

What happens downstream when a resident submits a request to the wrong department?
- How long does manual re-routing take in a typical city 311 system?
- What is the resident experience during this delay?
- In what scenarios could wrong routing cause real harm (e.g., urgent health/safety issues routed to a slow-moving department)?

Confidence calibration failure:
- What does the AI/NLP literature say about the risks of overconfident classification?
- How do production civic tech tools handle queries that fall outside their training distribution?
- What is the difference between "I don't know" (appropriate) vs. making a low-confidence guess (dangerous)?

Category ambiguity in Richmond's context:
- What are the categories in the RVA311 system most likely to be confused with each other?
- What situations genuinely span multiple departments (e.g., pothole + water main, graffiti on private property, noise from permitted event)?

Mitigation approaches:
- How do well-designed government service tools handle uncertain queries?
- What confidence threshold triggers a "please call 311" response?
- What disclaimer language effectively communicates uncertainty without destroying usefulness?

Output
- Specific wrong routing scenarios with downstream consequences
- Confidence calibration recommendations
- Required safety features (human fallback, source citation, confidence display)
- Non-negotiable language requirements for any routing suggestion
